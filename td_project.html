Designing Phishing Rod

A cybersecurity tool to educate the masses

The goal of this project was to create an app, and later web extention, that would sift through an attached email and separate phishing mail for your everyday important mail. The app's appeal was its AI link tester, which would test suspicious hyperlinks for the user to prevent the spread of malware, and the education feature, which would teach the user how to better protect themself from malicious actors and how to spot phishing mail.
The app was predicted to target young individuals, as the youth become more concerned about their online security, elderly individuals looking to learn some digital-age tricks, and businesses looking to train employees on the dangers of phishing emails and malware.
Our mission is to educate the world to protect themself online, as studies show that most breaches, hacks, and other threats to one's cybersecurity are caused by individuals not knowing how to differentiate normal advertisement spam from malicious mail looking to infiltrate a company or steal private information from a loved one. If we educate them, then they can fight back without having to become a technology wizard.


Being a designer, I was brought on to be a designer with a background in programming. This maked me extra useful in that I can come up with solutions to design problems that look good, function well, and can be easy to program.
I conducted interviews with two college-aged students, two retired individuals, and a chief digital officer of a large tech company. This gave me some very good starting points for the layout and functional design that this app was going to utilize. Key features at this time were a recommended bottom screen nav-bar for phone users so as to not strain the thumb during navigation, bright ad distinguished colors for the older users to better navigate, and a dual-mailbox system available for users to use directly on the app.


Me and my team came up with two wireframes to showcase what we were thinking. One of these photos did get corrupted at some point, though.

You will notice in these wireframes, though that the bottom nav-bar is present here at the very early stage. Since there wasn't much functional to the home page outside of navigation, we attempted to keep everything that would take you to another page in the app, close to the bottom. We also made the buttons really big, foreseeing that making everything a different bright color would hurt those who can see. The alternative was to make things bigger and easier to read.
We later came up with a color scheme of red vs. blue with a flow-y wave style layout to give a calm feel to the app, make you feel safe here.


For prototyping, we use Figma. When we began prototyping, we decided to try a version where the nav-bar was instead a drop-down menu that was located towards the top of the page. This was to see how many people in our first user test would opt for the bottom navigation style. Please note: the image of Prototype A is the final form it took. We did not manage to save any pictures of Prototype A prior to the evaluation.

There wasn't much of a difference due to internal approval for what was dubbed "Version A." This was the one the higher-ups most approved of and actively pushed. As explained earlier, one has a bottom nav-bar, and one has a drop-down menu near the mid-to-top of the display.
We then moved on to our first user evaluation, where the long-asked question of which nav-menu would be liked most. We conducted a "think-aloud" procedure during the evaluation, where the users were asked to speak out loud about what they were thinking, seeing, or experiencing. This would give us the rawest opinion possible from our test users. We came out of that evaluation with the user insight on a few things. First, users preferred the bottom nav-bar better. This pretty much meant that the foundation of prototype B was gone, however, the original Prototype A was a lot more cluttered design-wise. Users preferred the minimalistic look of Prototype B over Prototype A. Equipped with this knowledge, we took the minimalistic design of B, modified it to fit with a bottom nav-bar, and then moved forward with Prototype A.
When we did our second evaluation, we did mostly the same thing as the first, just with Version A only. From that evaluation, we learned that users wanted a shortcut from the mailbox to the spam mail, or "Fresh Catch" box, and vice versa. A few users recommended a tile menu on the profile page instead of a column menu. Finally, we were also told that the settings panel was currently very lacking. This is due to the limitations of Figma, but as we move towards our home-built app, that will be fixed.


To wrap up this reflection of my time working on Phishing Rod, I like to say that Figma is a great tool to create a working and interactive experience for the users. It was easy to use once you got the hang of hotkeys and learned a few tricks. If I had to change anything, I'd have kept more screenshots of things like wireframes and prototypes. I get the feeling that I have a bad habit of latching onto one model and running with it before others are made or even considered. I feel that there was a lot of that in this project from me. What I learned from this experience is that being on the design side of things is not like being on the programming side. Things aren't being designed as you go; limitations are hard to predict at this stage, and there is a reason these things are planned out before starting. I usually design my websites on the fly because I like getting hands-on with the code. This has taught me that maybe taking a step back would make the coding experience more streamlined.
